Backstory in Short

Made up of 3 penguin conservation groups, the Purple Penguin Project is dedicated to hosting a festival held on World Penguin Day (April 25th) each year. This festival takes place in Maimi, FL, and its purpose is to bring awareness to, and raise money for, the conservation and care of all endangered species of penguins around the world. All of the money raised will go to any of the 3 penguin conservation groups that make up the Purple Penguin Project to aid them in their conservation efforts.

Endangered Penguins

Galapagos Penguin

(Spheniscus mendiculus)

The Galapagos penguin is a penguin endemic to the Galapagos Islands, Ecuador, making it the only penguin found north of the equator. It is the second smallest species of penguin, and with an estimated population of 1,800 it is currently the rarest penguin species.

Threats to the species include climate change, disease, human harvesting of penguins for oil and other products, competition with fisheries for krill and other fish, habitat loss, increased predation from invasive species, bycatch, and pollution.

Erect-Crested Penguin

(Eudyptes sclateri)

The erect-crested penguin is a penguin endemic to the New Zealand region. It is a small-to-medium sized penguin whose current population is estimated at 150,000.

The main threat to this species are the drastic declines in population that have occurred over the last several decades. Because its biology is poorly studied, there is little known about what may be causing these population declines.

Yellow-Eyed Penguin

(Megadyptes antipodes)

The yellow-eyed penguin is a penguin endemic to New Zealand, and it may be the most ancient of all living penguins. It is the largest living penguin to breed on the mainland of New Zealand with an estimated population of 4,000.

Threats to the species include habitat degradation, introduced predators, and disease.

Northern Rockhopper Penguin

(Eudyptes moseleyi)

The northern rockhopper penguin is a penguin native to the southern Indian and Atlantic Oceans. It is larger than the other two rockhopper penguin subspecies and has an estimated population between 100,000-499,999.

Threats that may have contributed to its population decline of over 90% since the 1950s include climate change, changes in marine ecosystems, and overfishing for squid and octopus by humans.

African Penguin

(Spheniscus demersus)

The African penguin is a penguin confined to southern african waters. The males in this species of penguin are slightly larger than the females and the estimated population is between 150,000-180,000.

Threats to the species include historical exploitation, oil spills, and competition with fisheries for food.
